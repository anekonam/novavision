# NovaVision — Company & Therapy Overview (Detailed)

## Company Profile

NovaVision is a medical technology company headquartered in Boca Raton, Florida, with a partner company in Germany. Their mission is to improve vision in patients with neurological visual impairments resulting from stroke or brain injury. They offer a portfolio of FDA cleared and registered therapies and diagnostic products, grounded in the principle of neuroplasticity — using repetitive stimulation to reactivate and strengthen residual visual function in damaged brain areas.

Their product portfolio spans three categories: **Restoration**, **Compensation**, and **Diagnostics**.

## 1. Vision Restoration Therapy (VRT)

### Overview

VRT is NovaVision's flagship therapy and the only commercially available FDA-cleared therapy designed to restore vision loss due to neurological brain damage. It is a prescription therapy, meaning patients need a referral and a visual field test before starting. It does not require surgery or medication.

### Scientific Basis

The therapy is built on the concept of neuroplasticity. The traditional view in neurology was that the adult brain is essentially hardwired after early childhood and cannot meaningfully recover function after injury. More recent research has demonstrated that the brain retains significant plasticity throughout adulthood, and that targeted, repetitive stimulation can reactivate damaged neural pathways.

When the brain is injured (through stroke, trauma, inflammation, or surgery), the neural networks that process vision are disrupted. However, some of these networks are duplicated in the brain. VRT exploits this by systematically stimulating the damaged area with light stimuli at specific locations, enabling the brain to activate alternative routes for processing visual information — essentially building a bypass mechanism around the damaged tissue.

The specific target of the stimulation is the "transition zone" — the border region between the intact visual field and the blind area. This zone contains residual neuronal structures that have survived the injury but are not functioning at full capacity. By repeatedly activating these cells through the course of therapy, VRT is designed to improve their neuronal efficacy, allowing patients to regain useful vision in areas that were previously part of the deficit.

### How the Therapy Works — Phase by Phase

**Phase 1: Diagnostics**
Before therapy begins, the precise limits and boundaries of the patient's vision loss must be mapped. This is done using High-Resolution Perimetry (HRP), where small white light dots are briefly presented on a grid covering the central ±20 x ±15 degrees in random locations. The patient responds each time they detect a dot. This task is repeated three times and the results are averaged. The output is a detailed map of visual sensitivity across the tested field, along with reaction time data for each point.

**Phase 2: Stimulation (Active Therapy)**
The patient undergoes repeated light stimulation targeted at the areas of impaired visual field that showed residual vision in the diagnostic phase — the areas most likely to respond to treatment. Stimuli are white, suprathreshold lights (0.15° diameter) shown against a dark background. A chin support is used for head stability and the monitor is placed at approximately 30cm distance.

During each session, the patient focuses on a central fixation point on the screen. Light stimuli are then presented repetitively in the transition zones. The patient responds each time they see a stimulus. Light stimuli parameters — size and brightness — are determined individually based on the patient's unique deficit profile.

Fixation control is maintained throughout: the central fixation point intermittently changes colour or shape, and the patient must respond to each change. Failure to respond to a fixation change is recorded as a fixation accuracy issue; pressing the button when no stimulus is presented is recorded as a false alarm. These quality parameters are monitored throughout.

The therapy is personalised and updated monthly by NovaVision clinical staff based on the patient's progress data.

**Phase 3: Evaluation**
At the end of the therapy course, High-Resolution Perimetry is repeated using the same protocol as the initial diagnostic. The pre-therapy and post-therapy visual field maps are compared to quantify the degree of improvement.

### Protocol and Commitment

- Performed at home on the patient's own computer
- Twice daily sessions (approximately 30 minutes each)
- Six days per week
- Standard course is six months
- Extensions are available (some patients have continued to 8–9 months with continued improvement)
- Monthly progress reports generated by NovaVision clinical staff
- Therapy can be done on the patient's own schedule

### Clinical Evidence

- Supported by 15+ years of research and over 20 published clinical studies
- 302-patient study showed notable improvements in over 70% of patients
- Average visual field expansion of approximately 4.9 degrees (Mueller I, et al., 2007; Romano JG, et al., 2008)
- Efficacy is independent of lesion age — patients with injuries from years or decades earlier still respond
- Improvements are reported as permanent and not influenced by age or gender
- The approximate 5-degree improvement in central vision has been shown to translate into meaningful functional gains in daily living: reading, navigation, watching television, socialising comfortably (Gall C, et al., 2008)

### Controversies and Critical Reviews

Some academic reviews have raised concerns about the robustness of VRT's evidence:

- **Fixation control concerns:** Patients with homonymous field defects naturally compensate by making frequent saccades towards their scotoma. The fixation monitoring method (detecting a colour change in the fixation light) has been criticised on the grounds that the colour transition is easy enough to detect without strict foveal fixation. Critics argue patients may learn to make small (approximately 5°) eye movements into their blind field while still detecting the fixation change, meaning the measured improvement may partially reflect compensatory eye movements rather than genuine field restoration.
- **Mechanism questions:** For patients with complete hemianopia (where the intact and damaged occipital lobes are physically separate), critics have questioned why stimulation along the vertical meridian should produce field recovery — no fringe of salvageable tissue exists in such cases.
- **Cross-condition applicability:** VRT was reported as effective for both monocular optic nerve lesions and homonymous post-chiasmal lesions. Some reviewers have noted difficulty conceiving of a single physiological mechanism that could explain improvement at different levels of the visual system.
- **Historical precedent:** Zihl and von Camon reported similar visual training improvements in the 1980s, which were later attributed to poor fixation control.

Key critical sources:
- Horton JC. "Disappointing results from Nova Vision's visual restoration therapy." British Journal of Ophthalmology. PMC1772467.
- Bischoff P et al. "NovaVision: vision restoration therapy." Current Opinion in Ophthalmology. PMID: 17065915.

### Conditions Treated by VRT

- Homonymous hemianopia (loss of half the visual field on the same side in both eyes)
- Quadrantanopia (loss of a quarter of the visual field)
- Visual field deficits from ischaemic or haemorrhagic stroke
- Visual field deficits from traumatic brain injury (TBI)
- Vision loss following brain surgery or tumour removal
- Vision loss from optic nerve damage
- Diffuse field defects including tunnel vision caused by tumour, brain surgery, or brain disorders
- Vision loss from inflammation affecting the brain

## 2. NeuroEyeCoach

### Overview

NeuroEyeCoach is NovaVision's compensation therapy. While VRT aims to restore lost visual field, NeuroEyeCoach takes a different approach: it accepts the current extent of the patient's vision loss and trains them to make the most of what remains by improving their eye movement strategies. The two therapies are complementary and are offered together in a combined suite.

### Scientific Basis

After stroke or brain injury, patients frequently lose not only part of their visual field but also the ability to make effective eye movements. Normal daily tasks — navigating environments, finding objects, reading, crossing roads — all depend on rapid, efficient scanning eye movements (saccades). When both the visual field and the eye movement system are impaired simultaneously, the combined effect on daily functioning is severe.

The compensatory training approach was originally developed by Professor Zihl in 1988 and has been used in various modified forms across 13 published studies involving 551 patients with homonymous visual field loss. The core finding across this body of work is that systematic saccadic training produces significant improvements in visual search performance, accompanied by more efficient oculomotor strategies and a measurable reduction in visual disability.

This approach has been shown to be superior to several alternatives, including practice with reading alone (Schuett et al., 2012), non-specific visual training (Roth et al., 2009), standard occupational therapy (Mödden et al., 2012), and counselling focused on coping strategies (Zihl, 2011).

As with VRT, the patient's age and the time elapsed since their brain injury do not significantly affect the treatment outcome (Zihl, 2011; Schuett and Zihl, 2012).

### How the Therapy Works

NeuroEyeCoach uses the Visual Search Task psychological paradigm. During each training session, the patient is presented with a computer screen displaying a number of items. They must search the screen and decide whether a particular target item is present amongst distractor items.

The level of difficulty is systematically altered by changing three variables:

1. **Target positions** — where on the screen the target appears, progressively moving into areas the patient finds harder to reach
2. **Number of distractors** — increasing the visual clutter the patient must search through
3. **Similarity between target and distractors** — making the discrimination task progressively harder

The programme is self-adaptive: it continuously adjusts the task difficulty to match the patient's current deficits and stage of recovery, while consistently encouraging more efficient eye movement patterns. The system has 12 levels of increasing difficulty.

The training effectively teaches patients to:
- Rapidly and continuously scan their surroundings
- Direct their gaze towards the blind field
- Bring previously unseen objects within their sighted field
- Re-integrate left and right visual information
- Build confident, efficient scanning habits for daily life

### Protocol and Commitment

- Performed at home on a computer (also available in clinical settings via the Centre Model)
- Each session takes approximately 15–20 minutes
- Minimum of 36 sessions across the 12 levels
- Can be completed in as little as 2–3 weeks if performed daily
- Self-adaptive — requires limited clinician time or intervention
- Pre- and post-therapy evaluation options are built into the programme to quantify the effect

### Clinical Evidence

- Based on over 30 years of research since the original Zihl protocols (1988)
- Validated in a peer-reviewed 300-patient study published in Cortex (Sahraie A, Romano JG, Cederblad MH, Kenkel S, 2020) — the largest eye movement training study ever published
- Results showed 87% improvement in search time and 80% of patients making fewer errors
- Outcomes were not dependent on age, gender, side of blindness, age at onset of brain injury, or time elapsed since injury
- Three-centre study (University of Aberdeen, University of Verona, University of Munich) published in BioMed Research International concluded NeuroEyeCoach is the first evidence-based, gold standard registered medical device of its type accessible to patients at home or in clinical settings

### Key Published Studies

1. Sahraie A, Romano JG, Cederblad MH, Kenkel S. "Efficacy and Predictors of Recovery of function after eye movement training in 296 hemianopic patients." Cortex 125 (2020) 149–160.
2. Sahraie A, Smania N, Zihl J. "Use of NeuroEyeCoach to improve eye movement efficacy in patients with homonymous visual field loss." BioMed Research International.

### Practical Significance

The clinical results mean patients using NeuroEyeCoach are able to detect objects and hazards they would otherwise have missed entirely, and they find these things significantly faster than before training. In daily life this translates to improved navigation, safer pedestrian and road behaviour, better reading, and reduced startle responses from unexpected people or objects appearing from the blind side.

## 3. VIDIT (Vision Diagnostic Test)

### Overview

VIDIT is NovaVision's clinical diagnostic screening tool. It is designed for use in rehabilitation centres, clinics, and hospitals rather than for direct patient use at home.

### How It Works

VIDIT uses high-resolution campimetric testing of the central 20 degrees of the visual field. The patient fixates on a central point while stimuli are presented at various positions across the central visual field. Their responses are recorded to build a map of visual field sensitivity.

### Purpose and Clinical Rationale

Visual field deficits after stroke or brain injury frequently go undetected because visual field testing has not historically been part of routine post-stroke or post-injury evaluations. An undetected visual field deficit can adversely affect other rehabilitation modalities (e.g. physiotherapy, occupational therapy, cognitive rehabilitation), because the patient may be failing tasks not because of the targeted impairment but because they cannot see properly.

Early detection through VIDIT screening allows clinicians to identify vision problems early in the rehabilitation pathway and either treat them directly (via VRT or NeuroEyeCoach) or account for them in the design of other therapies.

### Protocol

- Tests can be performed in less than 10 minutes (approximately 7 minutes reported)
- Designed for use by rehabilitation therapists and healthcare professionals
- Requires minimal clinician training to administer
- Part of the NovaVision Centre Model package

## 4. InVista

### Overview

InVista is an in-clinic therapy tool based on the same principles as VRT but designed for supervised use within rehabilitation centres rather than unsupervised home use.

### How It Works

InVista is an automated training stimulation programme that presents light stimuli interactively according to the patient's responses. Like VRT, it targets the transition zone between intact and damaged visual field, but in a controlled clinical environment where a therapist can monitor the session directly.

### Purpose

InVista serves two main purposes:

1. **Therapeutic stimulation** — providing restoration-type therapy to patients who are in-clinic (e.g. during an inpatient rehabilitation stay) rather than at home
2. **Residual function identification** — helping clinicians identify the presence and extent of residual visual functions in the damaged field, which can inform treatment planning

### Availability

InVista is part of the NovaVision Centre Model and is offered alongside VIDIT and NeuroEyeCoach Professional to rehabilitation centres and clinics.

## 5. Online Visual Field Screening Test

### Overview

NovaVision offers a free online visual field screening test that patients can complete at home on their own computer. This is not a diagnostic tool — it is a preliminary screening designed to flag potential vision problems that may warrant further clinical investigation.

### How It Works

The test takes approximately 3–5 minutes. The patient maintains focus on a fixed central point on the screen while stimuli are presented in different areas of their peripheral vision. They press a key each time they detect a stimulus or when the central fixation point changes. The programme tracks which stimuli are detected and which are missed, building a picture of visual field sensitivity.

### Limitations

The results are informational rather than diagnostic. Variables such as screen calibration, ambient lighting, fatigue, and attention levels all influence online test results in ways that controlled clinical testing minimises. The test cannot definitively identify the cause or extent of any vision changes.

### Purpose

The value of the screening lies in accessibility. Patients can complete a test when concerns arise without waiting for a clinical appointment. Results can help guide conversations with healthcare providers about whether comprehensive visual field testing is needed. It also helps patients determine whether they may be eligible for VRT.

## How the Therapies Work Together

NovaVision's recommended approach is to use VRT and NeuroEyeCoach together in their combined therapy suite ($900). The rationale is straightforward:

- **VRT** works on the restoration side — actually expanding the visual field by activating residual neurons in the transition zone. This is a slow, intensive process (6 months) targeting genuine neurological recovery.
- **NeuroEyeCoach** works on the compensation side — training the patient to make better use of whatever visual field they have (whether that's their pre-VRT baseline or their improved post-VRT field). This is a faster process (2–4 weeks) targeting behavioural adaptation.

The two therapies address different aspects of the overall visual impairment and the functional benefits compound. A patient who has both a slightly larger visual field (from VRT) and significantly better scanning strategies (from NeuroEyeCoach) will experience a greater practical improvement in daily life than either therapy alone would deliver.

For clinical settings, the Centre Model packages VIDIT (for initial screening), InVista (for in-clinic restoration), and NeuroEyeCoach Professional (for in-clinic compensation) as a complete in-patient visual rehabilitation workflow.
